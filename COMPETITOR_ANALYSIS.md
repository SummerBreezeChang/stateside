# Stateside Competitor Analysis

**Research date:** July 21, 2026  
**Question:** What already helps international students find or qualify for housing, and which decision problems remain fragmented?

## Executive conclusion

The housing journey is well served in pieces:

- Large marketplaces provide inventory, filters, tours, applications, and payments.
- Student-specialist marketplaces provide purpose-built accommodation and assisted booking.
- University portals provide institution-linked inventory, roommate search, commute information, and renter education.
- Guarantor products help selected applicants qualify.
- Roommate platforms match people using self-reported lifestyle filters.
- Universities publish credible but largely static guidance.

The gap is between **shortlisting and committing**. No reviewed product combines a student's academic dates, rest/privacy needs, transportation reality, international rental readiness, property evidence, and lease tradeoffs across places from different sources—then turns the comparison into specific questions and verification steps.

Stateside should occupy this decision layer. It should complement marketplaces, universities, and guarantor services rather than trying to replace them.

## Competitive landscape

| Category | Examples | What they solve well | What remains fragmented |
|---|---|---|---|
| General rental marketplace | Zillow, Apartments.com | Broad inventory, search filters, favorites, tours, applications, messages, payments | Newcomer-specific qualification paths; program/lease alignment; cross-platform evidence comparison; rest and roommate unknowns |
| Student accommodation marketplace | Student.com, amber | Student-oriented inventory, room types, proximity, booking support, tenancy coordination | Any-source comparison; U.S.-specific approval variability; independent tradeoff analysis before booking; visible evidence/unknown ledger |
| International/medium-term marketplace | HousingAnywhere | Remote renting, landlord messaging, online booking, first-payment protection | Deep U.S. student readiness; comparison of outside listings; campus-life and rest fit; academic-date decision support |
| University housing portal | Off Campus Partners and university-specific portals | University-branded listings, roommates, renter resources, commute and pricing information | Coverage depends on institution and participating inventory; limited cross-source personalization; no integrated qualification/verification plan |
| Lease guarantor | TheGuarantors, U.S. Guarantors, Homebody | Helps renters without standard credit, income, or a personal guarantor qualify at participating properties | Does not determine whether the home fits; used after a property is chosen; cost and acceptance vary; does not verify unrelated listings |
| Roommate matching | Roommates.com, university roommate finders | Search by location, budget, identity verification, hobbies, smoking, and general lifestyle | Detailed sleep/guest/cleaning agreement; lease readiness; academic dates; property and payment verification |
| University/international office guide | International student services and off-campus living offices | Credible local rules, scam warnings, referrals, and checklists | Static and distributed; requires the student to translate advice into a specific personal decision |
| Maps and transit | Google Maps and local transit tools | Routes, distance, nearby essentials, some departure-time planning | Does not connect the route to class schedule, late-evening reliability, car dependency, lease, cost, or qualification |

## Competitor profiles

### Zillow

**Position:** A broad U.S. rental ecosystem.

**Current strengths:** Zillow's Renter Hub lets users search, save favorites, message landlords, apply, complete leases, pay rent, and access renter guides. Its reusable application supports participating rentals for a single fee during a 30-day period. Zillow also offers virtual tours and walkthrough guidance. ([Zillow Renter Hub](https://www.zillow.com/rent/renter-hub/), [Zillow applications](https://www.zillow.com/rent/apply-for-rentals/))

**International-student limitation:** The standard application experience is organized around U.S. residence, income, credit, background, and SSN-linked screening. It is an efficient transaction tool once a renter and property fit the system; it does not explain property-specific alternatives for an incoming student who lacks those records. ([Zillow application process](https://www.zillow.com/rentals-network/how-to-zillow-rental-applications/))

**Open space for Stateside:** Bring Zillow listings into a cross-platform shortlist; calculate academic-date and total-cost mismatches; prepare no-SSN/credit questions before the student pays an application fee.

### Student.com

**Position:** A global student-accommodation marketplace focused heavily on purpose-built student housing.

**Current strengths:** Student.com advertises more than one million beds across 1,000 universities and 400 cities, with filters, maps, neighborhood guides, booking consultants, and tenancy coordination. It emphasizes managed student properties and remote booking support. ([Student.com](https://www.student.com/home/index))

**Limitation:** Its value is strongest inside its own inventory and booking process. Many international students also consider private landlords, university portals, Facebook groups, local property managers, and sublets that sit outside the platform.

**Open space for Stateside:** Act as a source-neutral decision layer across a Student.com option and alternatives elsewhere; make personal tradeoffs and missing evidence comparable.

### amber

**Position:** Student accommodation discovery and assisted booking.

**Current strengths:** amber supports shared rooms, private rooms, studios, and apartments; lets students shortlist properties; uses booking experts; coordinates documents, contracts, guarantors, deposits, and post-booking support. ([Room selection](https://help.amberstudent.com/portal/en/kb/articles/how-to-find-the-right-room), [Booking process](https://help.amberstudent.com/portal/en/kb/articles/booking-process))

**Limitation:** The flow is oriented toward converting an inquiry into a booking within amber's property network. Its own help center says guarantors are essential for booking but amber can provide a guarantor service to eligible students. That is useful transaction support, but it is not an independent comparison of all options a student has gathered.

**Open space for Stateside:** Help the student decide whether an amber property, private rental, roommate arrangement, or university-linked option best fits the actual stay before entering any seller's booking funnel.

### HousingAnywhere

**Position:** International medium- and long-term rental marketplace, particularly strong in Europe.

**Current strengths:** Direct landlord messaging, remote booking, payment handling, and tenant protection for transactions on the platform. The company began as a student-to-student housing forum and expanded into a broader rental marketplace. ([About HousingAnywhere](https://housinganywhere.com/about), [Renting process](https://housinganywhere.com/renting), [Tenant protection](https://answers.housinganywhere.com/en/articles/2499070-what-are-the-advantages-of-booking-a-place-on-housinganywhere))

**Limitation:** Protection and workflow benefits apply to marketplace transactions. The platform is not primarily a U.S. international-student readiness product and does not analyze arbitrary outside options against academic and rest priorities.

**Open space for Stateside:** Source-neutral U.S. decision preparation before transaction, with explicit qualification and campus-life analysis.

### Off Campus Partners

**Position:** University-branded off-campus housing portals, now part of Apartments.com/CoStar.

**Current strengths:** University-linked listings, roommate finding, renter education, immersive tours, fee clarity, location details, and commute information. OCP describes its platform as a trusted, university-supported experience from search through move-in. ([Off Campus Partners](https://www.offcampuspartners.com/grow/ocp))

**Why it is the strongest adjacent competitor:** OCP already combines trusted inventory, student context, roommates, pricing, commute, and education. It can reduce many of the same uncertainties Stateside targets.

**Remaining gap:** Access and depth vary by partner institution and listing participation. The product is fundamentally a housing portal, while Stateside can analyze places gathered from any source and focus deeply on incoming international-student qualification, evidence gaps, academic-date fit, and personalized next actions.

**Strategic implication:** Position Stateside as a potential companion or future university integration—not as a claim that university portals are inadequate.

### TheGuarantors and related qualification services

**Position:** Lease-guarantee coverage that helps applicants qualify while protecting landlords.

**Current strengths:** TheGuarantors says it can consider non-U.S. citizens, students, renters without an SSN, and renters without a credit score. Its renter purchases a policy that insures the landlord, with pricing personalized to the application and property. ([Eligible applicants](https://help.theguarantors.com/what-kinds-of-applicants-can-you-approve-for-rent-coverage), [Renter product](https://www.theguarantors.com/renter))

**Limitation:** A guarantor product solves one approval route after the renter has a property in mind, and the property must accept or participate in the arrangement. It does not compare daily-life fit, lease mismatch, property evidence, or alternative qualification routes.

**Open space for Stateside:** Explain guarantor services as one possible route, ask whether the property accepts them, compare the cost/obligation with other routes, and never imply approval.

### Roommates.com and university roommate finders

**Position:** Room and roommate discovery.

**Current strengths:** Roommates.com offers filters including ID verification, move-in date, rent, university, age, pets, smoking, and interests/personality badges. ([Roommates.com search](https://www.roommates.com/help/search-outside-your-matches))

**Limitation:** Search filters do not replace a specific pre-move expectations conversation about sleep, guests, cleaning, shared bills, storage, communication, and conflict.

**Open space for Stateside:** Generate a neutral roommate living agreement and show compatibility unknowns as part of the housing decision without making cultural or demographic assumptions.

## The unresolved jobs worth solving

### 1. Compare options from different ecosystems

Students commonly use several sites and informal groups. Zillow's 2025 renter research found the typical recent renter used five sites/apps during a search. ([Zillow Consumer Housing Trends](https://www.zillow.com/research/renters-housing-trends-report-2025-35647/))

No single marketplace is motivated to make an outside listing look better than its own inventory. A source-neutral comparison is therefore structurally differentiated.

### 2. Know the approval route before paying to apply

Competitors either process a conventional application or sell a particular approval mechanism. Students still need to ask:

- Does this property require an SSN?
- Does it accept an I-20, stipend, sponsor, or proof of funds?
- Does it accept a foreign guarantor or commercial guarantor?
- Which provider is accepted, and what does it cost?

Stateside should prepare the question set and likely evidence route without predicting approval.

### 3. Connect the lease to the academic stay

Marketplace filters may include lease length, but they rarely explain the financial consequence of a twelve-month lease for a nine-month program or connect it to subletting and early-termination unknowns.

### 4. Treat rest as an educational requirement

Competitors filter amenities and general roommate lifestyle. The research revealed a deeper need: protect sleep, recovery, privacy, and study conditions. Stateside can make quiet hours, guests, sleeping arrangements, light, temperature, and commute fatigue explicit questions.

### 5. Convert unknowns into a verification plan

Platforms provide information and some verify participants, but an incoming student often brings options from outside those protected systems. Stateside can maintain an evidence ledger:

- Confirmed from supplied material
- Inferred and requiring confirmation
- Unknown
- Independently verified

Then it can generate the minimum steps needed before payment.

### 6. Explain tradeoffs without claiming certainty

Existing products commonly optimize search ranking or booking conversion. Stateside can optimize for decision preparedness: why a place fits, what conflicts with an essential need, and which missing fact could change the decision.

## Recommended positioning

### Category

**International student housing decision companion**

### One-line differentiation

> Marketplaces help you find a place. Stateside helps you understand the places you found before you commit.

### Product wedge

1. Bring two or three saved housing options from anywhere.
2. Add academic dates, budget, transportation, rest, privacy, and rental-readiness context.
3. Review extracted facts and preserve unknowns.
4. Compare tradeoffs across the same decision framework.
5. Receive a property-specific verification and inquiry plan.

### Defensible direction after the hackathon

- A growing library of school/city-specific rental timelines and qualification norms
- Evidence templates and question sets learned from resolved student decisions
- University/international-office partnerships
- Integrations that accept listings from any marketplace without owning inventory
- Outcome feedback about which unknowns changed decisions or caused post-move regret

## What Stateside should not build

- Another broad listing marketplace
- A direct-booking funnel
- Its own guarantor or tenant-screening product
- A proprietary neighborhood safety or crime score
- Automated legal conclusions about a lease
- An unverified landlord/property certification badge
- Roommate recommendations based on nationality, culture, or protected traits

## The compelling hackathon story

> International students do not suffer from a complete absence of housing tools. They suffer because each tool solves one slice: inventory, booking, maps, roommates, qualification, or advice. The student still has to combine those pieces while abroad, under deadline, without U.S. rental history or local knowledge. Stateside uses GPT-5.6 to turn a student's circumstances and several unstructured housing options into one explainable decision: what fits, what conflicts, what remains unknown, and what to verify next.

That story is credible because Stateside does not claim to replace mature marketplaces. It makes their information more useful at the moment of decision.
